The first scheduled intercity railway service on Earth began its maiden service on 15th September 1830 with great fanfare. The Prime Minster and former Liverpool MP, The Duke of Wellington, was guest of honour, and crowds gathered to watch the new-fangled trains set off from Edge Hill towards Manchester, with a scheduled stop just outside Newton-le-Willows.

The trains were slow by our standards, but exhilaratingly rapid for the people of the day whose greatest sensation of speed had been riding a horse. The incredible power and almost magical ability to produce it also left a deep impression on many.

## Fanny Kemble

Twenty-one-year-old actress Fanny Kemble was given a preview ride on the legendary Rocket locomotive a few weeks before the famous opening, and described the experience in her memoirs with a delightful novelty:

> You can't imagine how strange it seemed to be journeying on thus, without any visible cause of progress other than the magical machine, with its flying white breath and rhythmical, unvarying pace.
> The carriage … was set off at its utmost speed, thirty-five miles an hour, swifter than a bird flies (for they tried the experiment with a snipe). You cannot conceive what that sensation of cutting the air was; the motion is as smooth as possible, too. I could either have read or written; and as it was, I stood up, and with my bonnet off "drank the air before me." The wind, which was strong, or perhaps the force of our own thrusting against it, absolutely weighed my eyelids down.
> When I closed my eyes this sensation of flying was quite delightful, and strange beyond description; yet, strange as it was, I had a perfect sense of security, and not the slightest fear.
> - Fanny Kemble in her memoirs.

## The Trains

The carriages were divided such that each row of seating was essentially a pew with its own door on either side which opened directly to the outside world, not onto a corridor within the train. This clear safety issue would tragically cost a life almost immediately.

## The Accident

The serving MP for Liverpool, William Huskisson, was also on that first official journey. Two years prior he had fallen out with Lord Wellington and left his cabinet under a cloud, so despite some ill-health he was determined to take the opportunity to repair the relationship.

The two men were on a special carriage that rode ahead of the main procession of carriages with its own locomotive, the Northumbrian. George Stephenson himself was driving it. When the train stopped near Newton-le-Willows, the passengers saw an opportunity for a break and alighted directly from their 'pews', which of course led many to be standing on the second track that ran alongside their own.

## Added

Huskisson sought out Wellington and shook his hand, before a cry of warning went out that the second train carrying the remaining guests was approaching on the second line parallel to theirs. The options were clear: get back in the train, cross the second track quickly and get out of the way, or stand firmly against the Duke's special train and allow the second train to pass by, as there was enough space between the two trains.

Huskisson evidently panicked, and his indecision left him out of time to cross the second track to safety. He then tried to climb back over the door of his own carriage back into his seat, but because the door had not been latched, it swung open with Huskisson bent double over it, right into the path of the oncoming Rocket. Huskisson was horribly injured, and died late that evening. There is a fairly grand memorial to the event where it took place just east of Newton-le-Willows, which is passed by the modern trains with most passengers none the wiser.

The service was so ahead of its time that the steam engines could not climb Edge Hill from Lime Street under their own power and had to be pulled up by steam winches at the top of the hill.
